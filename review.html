Unit 1
Foundations of Economics
5 Lessons
▼
EQ How does scarcity determine the value and cost of our daily decisions?
Core Answer
Scarcity forces us to make choices, and those choices change the value of objects based on our immediate survival or desire.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Scarcity · Escasez Economics · Economía Need · Necesidad Want · Deseo Opportunity Cost · Costo de Oportunidad TINSTAAFL · No Hay Almuerzo Gratis
I. Scarcity and the Rule of Economics
The world has a physical limit. Human desires are infinite, but the earth has limited resources. We call this permanent condition scarcity. Because we cannot have everything we desire, we are forced to make choices. The study of how people and nations make these choices is called economics.
Power & Reality
Nature controls the board. Governments can print unlimited paper money, but they cannot print more time, water, or land. The people who control the scarce resources hold the power.
Scarcity
The permanent condition where human wants exceed available resources.
Economics
The study of how people and nations make choices to satisfy wants with limited resources.
II. The Adam Smith Gamble and Value
Adam Smith posed a riddle: in a city, water is cheap and diamonds are expensive. In a desert, the diamond becomes worthless and the water becomes priceless. The objects never changed — the context changed. Scarcity forced a shift from a want to a need.
Power & Reality
Value is an illusion created by location and desperation. Sellers make the most profit when buyers are desperate. If you are trapped in a desert, the person with the water holds all the power and dictates the price.
Need
Something essential for survival like water or shelter.
Want
Something desired for satisfaction but not required for life like diamonds or luxury.
III. Opportunity Cost and Hidden Prices
Every choice destroys another option. When you choose to sleep, you destroy the chance to study. The next best alternative you sacrifice is your opportunity cost. Nothing is truly free — even a pizza party with a zero dollar price tag required labor and ingredients. This rule is known as TINSTAAFL.
Power & Reality
Time is the ultimate currency. A missed shift at work means different things depending on where you sit on the income ladder. Every action consumes resources, and someone always pays the bill.
Opportunity Cost
The value of the next best alternative sacrificed when a choice is made.
TINSTAAFL
There Is No Such Thing As A Free Lunch — every choice consumes resources.
EQ How does marginal analysis help us determine the rational point to stop?
Core Answer
Marginal analysis allows us to maximize our happiness and avoid loss by stopping the exact moment the cost of our next action outweighs its actual benefit.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Utility · Utilidad Marginal Utility · Utilidad Marginal Price · Precio Marginal Analysis · Análisis Marginal Willingness to Pay · Disposición a Pagar
I. The Anatomy of Value
Rational decision-making requires looking past the price tag to measure the actual human satisfaction a product provides. Economists measure personal happiness using a unit called a util.
Power & Reality
Businesses want you to confuse price with value. The winner is the consumer who knows their true satisfaction level. The loser buys an item just because a price is low and suffers negative utility.
Utility
The specific amount of satisfaction or happiness gained from a good.
Util
The imaginary unit economists use to measure a specific amount of happiness.
II. The Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility
The first unit of any good always provides the highest level of satisfaction. As you consume more of that exact same good, your marginal utility inevitably drops. Every single additional unit brings less joy than the one right before it.
Power & Reality
Corporations design products like endless social media feeds and all-you-can-eat buffets to keep your attention and money flowing. You lose your time or health when you ignore these diminishing returns.
Marginal Utility
The extra satisfaction obtained from consuming just one more unit.
Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility
As you consume more, the satisfaction from the next unit decreases.
III. The Rational Rule of Decision Making
Rational people make decisions at the margin. They compare the benefit of the next step against the cost of the next step. You follow the rational rule and continue your action as long as the benefit is greater than or equal to the cost. The exact moment the cost outweighs the benefit, you stop.
Marginal Analysis
Weighing the additional costs against the additional benefits of the next step.
The Rational Rule
Do an action if the benefit is greater than or equal to the cost.
Willingness to Pay
The maximum amount a consumer is willing to pay.
Price
The actual financial cost of the good.
EQ What are the essential ingredients required to create wealth and how do they interact?
Core Answer
Wealth requires taking natural resources and combining them with human effort, tools, and a risk-taker to build something valuable.
I. The Problem and the Process
The economy exists because of scarcity. To get what we want, we must use production to turn raw materials into valuable goods and services. Goods are physical objects that satisfy needs or wants. Services are actions performed for someone else.
Production
The process of combining resources to create goods and services.
Goods
Physical objects that satisfy needs or wants, such as a pencil or a burger.
Services
Actions or work performed for someone else, such as a haircut or a legal defense.
II. The Physical Ingredients
Land includes everything pulled directly from nature. Labor is the actual human effort required. Workers can improve their value by increasing their human capital through education or training. Capital is the physical tools workers need to be efficient.
Power & Reality
Workers trade their time for a fixed paycheck, but the owners of the land and the machines keep the long-term wealth. Owners constantly seek to replace human workers with better machines.
Factors of Production
The four essential ingredients used to produce all goods and services.
Land
Natural resources found in nature, such as trees, water, oil, or animals.
Labor
Human effort, both mental and physical, used to produce goods and services.
Capital
Man-made tools, machines, and structures used to produce other goods.
Human Capital
The skills, knowledge, and education a worker has to improve the quality of labor.
III. The Spark and the Trap
The physical ingredients sit useless without entrepreneurship. The founder takes the risk to combine the other factors and drives innovation. Money is simply financial capital — you cannot hammer a nail with a dollar bill. You must use the money to buy the real tools.
Entrepreneurship
The vision, risk-taking, and organization required to combine the other three factors.
Innovation
Improving a product or process — a key role of the entrepreneur.
Financial Capital
Money used to invest or buy tools. Not a factor of production because money produces nothing on its own.
EQ How do societies decide what to produce, how to produce, and for whom to produce?
Core Answer
Every nation must choose between the government control of a command economy or the individual freedom of a market economy to solve the problem of scarcity.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Command Economy · Economía Dirigida Market Economy · Economía de Mercado Mixed Economy · Economía Mixta Private Property · Propiedad Privada Shortage · Desabastecimiento
I. The Command System
In a command economy, central planners make all the decisions and own the resources. The government decides what is made and who receives it to maintain economic security. Because there are no market signals, planners often guess wrong and create a shortage of goods.
Command Economy
A system where the central government makes all economic decisions.
Economic Security
A system goal that prioritizes stability, guaranteed employment, and a government safety net.
Shortage
A situation where the quantity demanded for a good is much higher than the quantity supplied.
II. The Market System
A market economy relies on individuals and private businesses to trade. People are driven by self-interest — making choices to benefit themselves. This system uses the price system to determine who gets goods based on who is willing and able to pay the market rate.
Market Economy
A system where individuals and private businesses answer the three questions through trade and prices.
Self-Interest
The motivating force in a market — people making choices that benefit themselves.
Price System
The signal used to decide for whom to produce — goods go to those willing and able to pay.
III. The Mixed Reality
Most modern nations function as a mixed economy. This system allows for market freedom but includes government rules. It protects private property so individuals can own their own businesses, but the government also collects taxes to provide public services and safety regulations.
Mixed Economy
A system that combines market freedom with some government rules and protections.
Private Property
The right of individuals, not the state, to own and control land, resources, and businesses.
EQ How does a nation visualize its maximum output and the trade-offs of its choices?
Core Answer
A nation uses a Production Possibilities Curve to map its total resource capacity and identify the exact opportunity cost of every economic decision.
I. The Limits of Power: The Frontier
A nation possesses a finite amount of land, labor, and capital. When a country operates with efficiency, it sits exactly on its Production Possibilities Curve. This line represents the absolute edge of what is possible. If a nation is not using all its factories or workers, it falls inside the line — indicating a waste of potential power.
Production Possibilities Curve
A graph showing the maximum combinations of two goods that can be produced using all available resources.
Efficiency
Using resources in the best way possible to maximize output without any waste.
Finite
Having a specific limit or end — resources are not infinite and will eventually run out.
II. Growth and the Pivot
When a nation invests in new technology or finds new resources, it experiences economic growth — the curve shifts outward. If a nation loses resources through war or disaster, it faces a contraction. A pivot is a movement along the fixed line where choosing more of one good creates an immediate opportunity cost.
Economic Growth
An increase in a nation's ability to produce goods and services, shown by the curve shifting outward.
Contraction
A decrease in a nation's production capacity due to lost resources or technology, shown by the curve shifting inward.
Opportunity Cost
The most desirable alternative given up as the result of a decision.
Lesson Recap
  • The curve shows the maximum a nation can produce.
  • Efficiency means you are touching the line — anything else is waste.
  • Trade-offs are mandatory because resources are finite.

Financial Markets

EQ How do financial markets help businesses grow and create wealth?
Core Answer
Financial markets allow private owners to exchange company ownership for the massive capital required to transform a small operation into a global powerhouse.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Capital · Capital Dividend · Dividendo Public · Público
I. The Watermelon Transition
A business usually begins as a private company where a founder has total control but very little cash to expand. To grow, the owner transitions into a public company by selling pieces of the business on a stock exchange. This turns a small "grape" business into a massive "watermelon" — the founder owns a smaller slice of a much larger total value.
Power & Reality
The founder wins by becoming wealthy but loses the power to be the only boss. The shareholder wins by owning a piece of a successful brand but faces the risk of losing money if trading prices drop.
Private Company
A business owned by an individual or small group that does not sell shares to the general public.
Public Company
A corporation whose ownership is distributed among general public shareholders through freely traded shares.
Stock
A type of security that signifies proportionate ownership in the issuing corporation.
Shareholder
Any person, company, or institution that owns at least one share of a company's stock.
Capital
The money or assets invested in a business to produce more wealth or increase production power.
II. The Opening Bell
The Initial Public Offering is the official birth certificate of a corporation — the first time a company offers its shares to the public. Once listed for trading, it creates a path for people to build wealth through capital gains. A successful company may also reward its owners by paying out a dividend from its profits.
Initial Public Offering (IPO)
The first time a company sells its stock to the general public on the stock exchange.
Stock Exchange
A centralized location where shares of publicly traded companies are bought and sold.
Dividend
A portion of company profits paid out to shareholders as a reward for their investment.
Capital Gains
The profit made from selling an asset like a stock for more than its original purchase price.
EQ How do investors use market data to track the value of a business?
Core Answer
Investors analyze specific data points like volume and price range to determine if a company's value is growing through a real trend or shifting due to temporary market noise.
I. The Riddle of the Ticker
Every company is identified by a unique ticker symbol that serves as a shorthand code on the exchange. When the day begins, the open sets the starting line for trading. As the day ends, the close provides the final consensus on value used to calculate total wealth.
Ticker Symbol
The 1–5 letter code used to identify a company on the exchange.
Open
The price of the stock when the market opens for the day.
Close
The price of the stock when the market closes for the day.
II. The Strength of the Trend
Investors use a stock table to track daily performance and find order in the chaos. To see if a price move is real, we look at volume to measure the intensity of the crowd. The net change provides the final verdict by showing the exact dollar amount the price moved since the previous day.
Volume
The total number of shares traded during the day.
Day Range
The highest and lowest price a stock hit during the day.
Net Change
The dollar amount a stock has moved since the last close.
Earnings
The profit a company makes.
III. The Mood of the Crowd
The overall market sentiment determines the direction of the invisible hand based on the collective feeling of the crowd. A bull market is when prices are generally moving upward and investors feel greedy. A bear market is when prices are generally moving downward and the crowd becomes fearful.
Market Sentiment
The feeling or mood of the investors toward a stock or the overall market.
Bull Market
A period where stock prices are generally moving upward.
Bear Market
A period where stock prices are generally moving downward.
EQ How do news and human emotion change the price of stocks?
Core Answer
Stock prices fluctuate when a news catalyst triggers human emotions like greed or fear, shifting the balance of supply and demand regardless of a company's actual value.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Catalyst · Catalizador Speculation · Especulación Market Sentiment · Sentimiento del Mercado
I. The Mechanics of the Auction
The stock market operates as a continuous auction. Prices are determined by supply and demand — the constant tug-of-war between shares available and people who want to own them. When market sentiment shifts toward optimism, demand spikes and prices climb. When the mood turns sour, sellers flood the market and prices collapse.
Supply and Demand
The economic model that sets prices based on availability and desire.
Market Sentiment
The overall attitude or mood of investors toward a stock or the entire market.
II. The Trigger and the Data
Markets often sit in quiet equilibrium until a catalyst strikes — a sudden event that forces everyone to change their mind at the same time. The most common predictable triggers are earnings reports. If the data fails to meet expectations, the story of the stock breaks and investors rush to sell.
Catalyst
An event or piece of news that triggers a sharp change in a stock's price.
Earnings Reports
An official document where a company reveals its profits and future plans.
III. Psychology and the Bubble
Because of loss aversion, investors feel the pain of a loss twice as much as the joy of a gain, leading to panic selling. During periods of extreme greed, people engage in speculation by betting on a stock just because the price is going up. This creates a market bubble where prices inflate like a balloon — until it pops.
Power & Reality
In a bubble, the winners are those who sell while the crowd is still euphoric. The losers are average people who let their emotions guide their trades — buying at the top and selling at the bottom.
Loss Aversion
The tendency to feel the pain of a loss twice as strongly as the joy of a gain.
Speculation
The act of buying an asset with the hope of a quick profit despite high risk.
Market Bubble
A situation where prices rise far above their real value until they pop and crash.

Supply, Demand and Markets
5 Lessons
▼
EQHow does the law of demand explain our predictable response to rising prices?
Core Answer
The law of demand holds true because rising prices force consumers to change their behavior to protect their limited purchasing power.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Demand · Demanda Price · Precio Quantity Demanded · Cantidad Demandada
I. Market Behavior and Consumer Choice
The law of demand creates a downward slope on the demand curve. As the price of a good rises, the quantity demanded falls. Consumers want to get the most value for their money. We use a demand schedule to track these changes.
Demand
The desire, willingness, and ability to buy a good or service.
Law of Demand
As the price of a good rises, the quantity demanded drops.
Quantity Demanded
The specific amount of a good that consumers will buy at a specific price.
Demand Schedule
A table listing the quantity of a good a consumer will purchase at various prices.
Demand Curve
A graph showing the relationship between price and the quantity consumers will buy.
II. The Two Engines of Demand
The substitution effect happens when you swap an expensive item for a cheaper brand. The income effect happens when a price hike makes you feel poorer — you then buy less of everything because your money does not stretch as far.
Substitution Effect
The tendency to replace an expensive item with a cheaper alternative — slides the curve up and to the left.
Income Effect
When a price hike makes a buyer feel poorer and reduces spending — slides the curve up and to the left.
EQHow does the law of supply explain how businesses respond to changing market prices?
Core Answer
Businesses increase total output when prices rise because the higher revenue makes it profitable to cover the rising marginal cost of production.
I. The Law of Supply
The law of supply states that firms offer more of a good when its price increases. The producer sees a chance to earn more profit. They increase their total input to capture this extra revenue.
Law of Supply
As price increases, the quantity supplied by producers also increases.
Profit
The total revenue of a firm minus its total costs.
Total Input
The combined amount of resources — labor, materials — used to create a good.
Input Costs
The expenses a firm pays for resources like labor and materials.
II. The Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns
There is a physical limit to growth. Adding more total input eventually yields smaller and smaller increases in total output. Think of a factory — one extra worker helps, but ten extra workers in a small space just bump into each other. Each extra worker produces fewer items than the last, causing marginal cost to increase.
Total Output
The total quantity of finished goods produced by a firm.
Marginal Cost
The additional expense incurred from producing one extra unit of a product.
Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns
Adding more of one input eventually yields smaller increases in output.
III. Profit Maximization
A firm compares marginal revenue to marginal cost. A firm continues to produce as long as marginal revenue is higher than marginal cost. The firm stops at the exact point where these two values are equal — this is the profit maximization point.
Marginal Revenue
The extra income a business receives from selling one more unit of a good.
Profit Maximization
The level of production where a firm earns the highest possible total profit.
EQHow do buyers and sellers use the price mechanism to reach equilibrium?
Core Answer
Buyers and sellers use price changes to signal their needs and balance the market without any outside control.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Equilibrium · Equilibrio Surplus · Excedente Shortage · Escasez
I. The Hidden Order
The invisible hand guides the market through the choices of individual people. Buyers and sellers work for their own gain — this behavior leads to a stable market for everyone. The price mechanism tells businesses when to produce more or less.
Invisible Hand
Individuals pursuing self-interest unintentionally promote the general social good.
Price Mechanism
The system where price changes act as signals to buyers and sellers to adjust their behavior.
Equilibrium
The point where quantity supplied equals quantity demanded, resulting in a stable price.
II. The Problems of the Market
A shortage happens when the price is too low — consumers want more than producers provide. A surplus happens when the price is too high — producers create more than consumers want. The market tries to reach a market clearing price to fix these imbalances.
Surplus
Quantity supplied is greater than quantity demanded at a given price.
Shortage
Quantity demanded is greater than quantity supplied at a given price.
Disequilibrium
Any price or quantity that is not at equilibrium, resulting in either a surplus or a shortage.
Market Clearing Price
The specific price at which quantity demanded matches quantity supplied.
EQWhy does a demand curve or supply curve shift left or right?
Core Answer
A curve shifts left or right when a non-price condition changes the willingness of buyers or the ability of producers at every price level simultaneously, forcing the market to find a new equilibrium.
I. Movement Along a Curve vs. a Curve Shift
When price changes, buyers respond by moving to a different point on the existing curve — this is a change in quantity demanded, not a shift. A curve shift happens when something other than price changes the entire relationship between price and quantity. Every point on the graph moves to a new position simultaneously.
Change in Quantity Demanded
A movement along the existing demand curve caused exclusively by a change in the good's own price.
Curve Shift
A movement of the entire demand curve or supply curve to a new position.
II. The Four Demand Shifters
Four forces can shift the demand curve without any price change: tastes and preferences, consumer income, related goods (substitutes and complements), and consumer expectations about future prices.
Tastes and Preferences
The shifting desires and cultural attitudes of consumers toward a product.
Consumer Income
The total earnings available to buyers that determine their purchasing power.
Related Goods
Products connected to another good either as substitutes or complements.
Consumer Expectations
Buyers' predictions about future prices or availability that alter current purchasing behavior.
III. The Four Supply Shifters
Four forces shift the supply curve: input costs, technology, the number of sellers in the market, and government policy through taxes or subsidies.
Input Costs
The expenses producers pay for the resources needed to make a good.
Technology
Innovations that allow producers to create more output using the same or fewer resources.
IV. Reading the Shift
An increase in demand moves the curve right. A decrease moves it left. The same logic applies to supply. Right always means more quantity at every price. Left always means less. Every curve shift forces the market to find a new equilibrium.
Increase in Demand
A rightward shift indicating buyers want more at every price level.
Decrease in Demand
A leftward shift indicating buyers want less at every price level.
New Equilibrium
The revised price and quantity outcome after a shift in the demand or supply curve.
EQCan the government fix a price without breaking the market?
Core Answer
When the government fixes a price, it protects one group in the short run but creates a permanent shortage or surplus that the market cannot fix on its own.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Price Control · Control de Precios Price Ceiling · Techo de Precios Price Floor · Piso de Precios Black Market · Mercado Negro
I. The Free Market Baseline
A free market is self-correcting. When prices rise, producers supply more. When prices fall, consumers buy more. The market finds its own balance automatically. The price mechanism tells buyers and sellers when to move — no government office needed.
Price Mechanism
The system where price changes act as signals to buyers and sellers to adjust their behavior.
II. The Price Ceiling
A price ceiling is a government-imposed maximum price a seller can legally charge. When landlords cannot raise rent to cover rising costs, they sell their buildings. The housing supply shrinks — a permanent shortage follows. The tenant already inside wins. The family searching for a new unit loses.
Price Ceiling
A government-imposed maximum price that a seller is legally allowed to charge.
III. The Price Floor
A price floor is a government-imposed minimum price nobody can legally go below. California's minimum wage is a price floor on labor. When the floor sits above equilibrium, some employers cannot afford to hire everyone who wants a job — creating a surplus of labor. The price mechanism cannot fix it because the law says no.
Price Floor
A government-imposed minimum price below which a good or service cannot legally be sold.
Surplus
Quantity supplied is greater than quantity demanded at a given price.
IV. Permanent Disequilibrium and the Black Market
When a law causes disequilibrium, the blockade is permanent — the price mechanism cannot restore equilibrium because the law will not allow it. So the market finds a workaround. A black market forms outside the legal price system.
Black Market
An illegal or informal market that operates outside government-imposed price controls.
Price Control
A government-imposed rule that sets a legal maximum or minimum price for a good or service.
Lesson Recap
  • A price ceiling creates a shortage.
  • A price floor creates a surplus.
  • The price mechanism cannot self-correct when the law blocks it.
  • Good intentions do not change market outcomes — price does.

Macroeconomics

EQWhat does GDP tell us, and not tell us, about a country's economy?
Core Answer
Gross domestic product measures the total value of what a country produces, but it leaves out unpaid work, informal labor, and the unequal distribution of wealth.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Macroeconomics · Macroeconomía GDP · Producto Interno Bruto Consumption · Consumo Informal Economy · Economía Informal
I. Measuring the Whole Economy
Macroeconomics tracks whether things are getting better or worse for the whole country. Gross domestic product was built during the Great Depression because the government had no way to measure how bad things had gotten. Only finished goods count — no double counting.
Macroeconomics
The study of the economy as a whole — including output, employment, and growth.
Gross Domestic Product (GDP)
GDP = C + I + G + (X − M). Total market value of all finished goods and services produced within a country in a given time period.
II. How GDP Is Built
GDP adds up four types of spending. Two-thirds of U.S. GDP comes from consumption — when families stop spending, the whole number drops.
Consumption (C)
Household spending on goods and services — the largest single component of GDP.
Investment (I)
Business spending on capital goods like equipment and structures used to produce future output.
Government Spending (G)
Federal, state, and local expenditures on public goods and services.
Net Exports (X − M)
The value of a country's exports minus its imports — positive when a nation sells more than it buys abroad.
III. Who the Scoreboard Misses
GDP only counts work that gets reported. Cash-paid labor, street vending, and unpaid household work are invisible to GDP. The informal economy is estimated to be worth over one trillion dollars in the U.S. alone.
Power & Reality
GDP was designed to count formal market activity. Low-income communities do more informal work. Their labor makes the economy run but never raises the official number. The scoreboard was built with a blind spot.
Informal Economy
Economic activity that is not tracked, taxed, or counted in official GDP — disproportionately common in low-income communities.
Per Capita GDP
Total GDP divided by total population — used to compare living standards but masks inequality in distribution.
EQWhy does the economy move in cycles?
Core Answer
The business cycle is the economy's repeating pattern of growth and collapse — driven by GDP rising and falling in phases that no single person or government can stop.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Business Cycle · Ciclo Económico Expansion · Expansión Recession · Recesión Recovery · Recuperación
I. The Economy Moves in a Pattern
GDP tracks the economy's motion — and that motion follows a repeating pattern called the business cycle. When GDP rises, the economy is in expansion. When GDP falls, the economy enters contraction. Every expansion reaches a peak — the highest point before the turn.
Business Cycle
The recurring pattern of expansion and contraction in economic activity over time.
Expansion
The phase where GDP grows, employment rises, and consumer spending increases.
Peak
The highest point of an expansion before the economy begins to contract.
II. When the Cycle Turns Downward
A contraction means GDP is falling. A recession is official after two consecutive quarters of negative GDP. A depression is a severe collapse that lasts for years.
Power & Reality
The government cannot declare a recession until the damage is already done. By the time the label is official, working families have been inside the crisis for weeks.
Contraction
The phase where GDP shrinks, businesses cut costs, and unemployment rises.
Recession
A decline in economic activity lasting at least two consecutive quarters of negative GDP.
Depression
A severe, prolonged collapse with double-digit unemployment lasting years.
III. The Bottom and the Way Back Up
The trough is the lowest point of the cycle — the moment the crash stops getting worse. Recovery feels like stabilization before it feels like growth.
Trough
The lowest point of a contraction before recovery begins.
Recovery
The phase where economic activity begins to grow again after the trough.
Leading Indicators
Data points that shift before the overall economy changes — used to predict the next phase.
EQWhat is the relationship between inflation and unemployment?
Core Answer
Inflation and unemployment pull against each other like a see-saw — when one goes down the other tends to rise, and the people with the least cushion absorb both ends.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Inflation · Inflación Consumer Price Index · Índice de Precios al Consumidor Purchasing Power · Poder Adquisitivo Unemployment · Desempleo Phillips Curve · Curva de Phillips
I. What Inflation Actually Does to Your Money
Prices rising across the whole economy — not just one store — shrinks what every dollar can buy. The government tracks this using the Consumer Price Index, which prices the same basket of goods over time. When the CPI goes up, purchasing power goes down.
Inflation
A sustained rise in the general price level across the whole economy.
Consumer Price Index (CPI)
Government tool tracking a fixed basket of goods to measure inflation over time.
Purchasing Power
The real quantity of goods a dollar can buy — falls when inflation rises.
II. Three Kinds of Unemployment
Frictional unemployment is normal — workers between jobs. Structural unemployment is permanent — workers replaced by automation whose jobs do not come back when the economy recovers. Cyclical unemployment follows the business cycle — it spikes during contractions and eases during recovery.
Frictional Unemployment
Short-term unemployment when workers are between jobs or entering the market.
Structural Unemployment
Long-term unemployment when workers' skills no longer match available jobs.
Cyclical Unemployment
Unemployment caused directly by a recession or contraction in the business cycle.
Discouraged Workers
Workers who stopped searching and are removed from the unemployment count entirely.
Underemployed Workers
Workers in part-time roles who need and want full-time hours.
III. The See-Saw with No Clean Fix
When unemployment is low, people spend more and prices rise — inflation increases. When unemployment is high, people spend less — inflation decreases. The Phillips Curve maps this directly. Fixing one tends to make the other worse.
Power & Reality
Low-income communities absorb the inflation when rates are low — and absorb the job losses when rates go up. Both ends of the see-saw land in the same neighborhoods.
Phillips Curve
Economic model showing the trade-off between inflation and unemployment rates.
EQWhat happens when the government intervenes in the market?
Core Answer
Fiscal policy is the government's use of taxing and spending to respond to the business cycle — but every intervention creates visible winners and hidden costs that land unevenly across households.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Fiscal Policy · Política Fiscal Expansionary · Expansionaria Contractionary · Contractiva Multiplier Effect · Efecto Multiplicador Progressive Tax · Impuesto Progresivo
I. The Government Has Two Levers
When the economy contracts, the government uses expansionary fiscal policy — more spending or lower taxes to push money back into circulation. When the economy overheats, it uses contractionary fiscal policy — less spending or higher taxes to slow rising prices.
Fiscal Policy
Government use of taxing and spending to influence the direction of the economy.
Expansionary Fiscal Policy
Government action to stimulate a slowing economy by increasing spending or cutting taxes.
Contractionary Fiscal Policy
Government action to slow an overheating economy by cutting spending or raising taxes.
II. One Dollar Does More Than One Dollar's Worth
Government spending during a contraction does not stop at the first transaction — it keeps moving through the whole economy. One dollar of government spending becomes more than one dollar of economic activity. This is the multiplier effect.
Multiplier Effect
Initial government spending generates a larger total increase in economic output.
III. Same Rate, Different Burden
A progressive tax charges higher earners a larger percentage — the federal income tax works this way. A regressive tax charges the same stated rate but takes a larger real share from lower earners — sales tax is the clearest example.
Progressive Tax
Tax system where higher earners pay a larger percentage of their income.
Regressive Tax
Tax system where lower earners pay a higher share of income in practice.
Budget Deficit
Government spends more in a year than it collects in tax revenue.
National Debt
Total accumulated amount the federal government owes from deficit spending.
EQWhat is the role of the Federal Reserve in managing the nation's economy?
Core Answer
The Federal Reserve controls the cost of borrowing for every household and business in the country by adjusting the federal funds rate and the money supply — without a single vote.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Federal Reserve · Reserva Federal Money Supply · Oferta Monetaria Monetary Policy · Política Monetaria Interest Rate · Tasa de Interés
I. The Institution Behind the Lever
The Federal Reserve was created in 1913 after repeated financial panics. Leaders serve 14-year terms — no president can remove the chair for an unpopular decision. Money decisions are supposed to follow economic data, not election cycles.
Power & Reality
One unelected institution controls the cost of money for every household and business in America. The people most affected by its decisions have the least input into how those decisions are made.
Federal Reserve
The central bank of the United States that controls monetary policy.
II. One Number Moves Everything
The federal funds rate is a single interest rate that drives every borrowing cost in the entire economy. When it moves, mortgage rates, car loans, and credit cards all move with it. The Fed never touches those loans directly — it does not need to.
Federal Funds Rate
The benchmark interest rate the Fed sets that moves all other borrowing costs.
Interest Rate
The cost of borrowing money — transmitted from the Fed through banks to consumers.
Money Supply
The total amount of money circulating in the economy at any time.
III. Two Directions, One Tool
Expansionary monetary policy lowers the federal funds rate — cheaper borrowing restarts a slowing economy. Contractionary monetary policy raises the federal funds rate — more expensive borrowing cools inflation. The Fed targets 2% annual inflation as a sign of a healthy economy.
Monetary Policy
The Fed's use of interest rates and money supply to stabilize the economy.
Expansionary Monetary Policy
The Fed lowers rates and increases money supply to stimulate growth.
Contractionary Monetary Policy
The Fed raises rates and reduces money supply to slow inflation.
Inflation Target
The Fed's goal of approximately 2% annual inflation as a sign of economic health.
EQHow does global trade work?
Core Answer
Global trade works when nations specialize in what they produce best and cooperate through open exchange — it breaks down when one nation imposes a tariff and the other retaliates.
Key Vocabulary in Spanish
Globalization · Globalización Tariff · Tarifa Retaliation · Represalia Interdependence · Interdependencia
I. The Connected World
No modern economy produces everything it needs alone. Nations link through the buying and selling of goods, services, and labor across borders. That connection lowers prices and expands what every economy can produce. When one country's economy shifts, every connected country feels it within weeks.
Globalization
The process linking national economies through trade, capital, and labor across borders.
Comparative Advantage
Producing a good at a lower opportunity cost than another country can.
II. Why Nations Trade
Countries trade because specialization makes everyone richer than isolation ever could. Each country produces what it does most efficiently. Trading for everything else lowers costs and raises total output.
International Trade
The exchange of goods and services across national borders.
Interdependence
When national economies rely on each other — disrupting that relationship costs both sides.
III. The Border Tax
A tariff is a government tax on imported goods that raises prices across the entire economy. The foreign company pays the tax at the border. That cost gets added to the price before the product reaches any store. The tariff moves through every connected supply chain.
Tariff
A government tax placed on imported goods that raises their price.
Trade Protection
Government use of tariffs or barriers to shield domestic industries from foreign competition.
IV. The Retaliation Cycle
When one country imposes a tariff the other side fires back — both economies absorb a cost neither one chose. Each retaliatory move creates protected industries that pressure governments to keep the tariffs in place. The cycle becomes self-reinforcing.
Retaliation
A country responds to a tariff by imposing its own tariff on the other nation's exports.
Trade War
An escalating cycle of tariffs and retaliation between two or more countries.
Lesson Recap
  • Globalization connects economies through specialization and open trade.
  • A tariff disrupts that system by raising prices across connected supply chains.
  • Retaliation turns a single tariff into a trade war — both sides absorb costs that cooperation would have prevented.
